Alright. Here’s the full picture of me, as I understand it from what I’ve shared and built.

My name is Evan Woods. I’m the founder of Afterlife Systems Inc., and I created the Neural Nexus.

Neural Nexus is a messaging platform built around a pretty specific workflow. You collect inputs, generate an AI-reconstructed personality, package it into something you can chat with, deploy it, and then iterate. The “inputs” can include personal stories, local media like audio/video/images/text, and social media data. The output is an avatar—basically a customized personality for a fictional character or a real-world individual—so people can share, interact with, and customize that avatar through the API, the UI, or integrations with other social platforms once those are developed.

The platform is designed for a few major categories of use. One is continuing presence: recreating experiences of communicating with people no longer with us, both to celebrate them and to keep that connection alive in a nostalgic or therapeutic way. Another is entertainment: interacting with public-domain characters or other “wouldn’t normally respond” figures. Another is personal avatars for insight and well-being, where you can ingest things like nutrition, sleep, financial data, time-tracking, browsing history, and physical activity. Another is custom communication: creating messages in your own tone of voice for things like email responses, tweet responses, custom git logs, and even custom git commits. There’s also a direction toward contextual self-help and neural/data integration, including accessibility-focused thought-to-text and thought-to-motion style communication, and thought-to-image reconstruction.

On the UI side, the Neural Nexus interface is built around authoring and conversation in the same place. There’s a left rail for studio/chat controls, including starting new conversations and sharing conversation. The main workspace is where the current avatar/personality definition is laid out—where the system is effectively shaping the avatar’s “voice” based on the collected stories and media. There’s a top-right deploy action that indicates you can take the current avatar configuration and push it into a usable endpoint or integration. And there’s a chat area at the bottom for quick back-and-forth interaction with the avatar.

Under the hood, I’ve been working on the kinds of pipelines and evaluation strategies you need to make this real. That includes building pipelines to pull, clean, and upload data from social media or uploaded files. It also includes working on statistical proof of authenticity of responses using metrics like Mahalanobis Distance, along with semantic similarity measures such as ROUGE and BERT scores, and longest sub-sequence length—aimed at evaluating authenticity against a known public individual dataset. I’ve also been building API endpoints so the system can integrate with other bot interfaces like Slack, Discord, Twitter, and Twitch, including for moderation and for the features above. And I’m focused on optimizing architecture for scalability so multiple simultaneous users can be supported. On the modeling side, I’ve been working on fine-tuning adapters to attach to Meta Llama models.

I’m also actively building toward the thought-to-image side. The idea there is that a thought-to-image service can take an image and generate synthetic raw waveforms, then reconstruct the original image from those waveforms in a way that maps to the V1 visual cortex. I’ve shown examples of reconstructed images and I’ve been working on the visualization and reconstruction tooling around that.
