# First Example
While enrolled in an edx MITx Course: Data Analysis Statistical Modeling and Computational Applications, I tasked with identifying who among a group of patients is diagnosed as either cognitively normal, under mild-cognitive impairment, or has Parkinson's Disease Dementia. I Extracted a BOLD signal from the fMRI images of the patients and computed the fractional Amplitude Low-Frequency Fluctuation scores of a multitude of brain regions before implementing spectral clustering to classify the three clusters of patients. As a result, I was able to identify brain regions that correlated to activity in the Right Substantia Nigra per group diagnosis, and I plot and deployed the correlated activity at https://efwoods.github.io/Parkinsons_Disease_Study/. The 16 page paper I wrote for the project concludes that cognitively normal patients have a right substantia nigra with activity that is similar to many other brain regions whereas patients with MCI or PDD have activity in the substantia nigra that is not correlated to any region (due to the lower fractional amplitude low-frequency fluctuations) or is correlated to the left caudate which is a primary part of the basal ganglia and a key component of motor coordination and planning.

# Second Example
While working at Boeing, I worked on an API Catalogue. One of the APIs I specifically worked on was a hyperledger-fabric blockchain airline part tracker. The problem was airline parts were not associated with documentation and as a result millions of dollars worth of airline parts were sitting unused and unable to be sold. I competed against an internal team to win a multi-million dollar contract with a proof-of-concept demo that showcased the immutable hyperledger-fabric ledger associating parts and moving parts through the parts lifecycle and worked with a product manager, architect, and team of developers to create a full-stack solution, and worked on the factory floor to acquire information on how the employees collected and used the associated airline part data. The project later included American Airlines and Nasa as vendors who would buy and sell parts.

# Third Example
I recently created a model that accepts an image of activation patterns of the full cortex from a kernel device and determines if an individual is actively laughing. I collected the data from a video of a podcast, identified segments where the individual was laughing, segmented the data, and trained the classifier using PyTorch. The model and data is available for public use as well as a Jupyter notebook detailing how to download and use the model. The model has an accuracy of 78% and the repository is located at: https://github.com/efwoods/kernel-brain-data
